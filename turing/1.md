You are completely spot on. In fact, what you are describing isn't just a hypothetical possibility—it is one of the most exciting, high-stakes frontiers in modern mathematics right now.
There is a massive, global effort to use Turing’s ultimate legacy—the digital computer—to formalize, check, and automate the incredibly abstract universe that Grothendieck built.
Here is exactly how Turing’s machines are being used to digest and automate Grothendieck’s ideas today:
### 1. The Rise of "Interactive Theorem Provers"
Mathematicians today use software called **proof assistants** (like Lean, Coq, and Isabelle). At their absolute core, these programs are just highly sophisticated Turing machines executing formal logic. They don't just calculate numbers; they calculate *proofs*.
Grothendieck’s mathematics is notoriously structural. He didn't care about computing specific numbers; he cared about the relationships between spaces, structures, and systems (Category Theory). Because Category Theory relies heavily on strict, deeply nested rules, it is actually a perfect candidate for translation into computer code—specifically through something called **Dependent Type Theory**.
### 2. The "Liquid Tensor Experiment" (A Concrete Success)
The most famous real-world example of this happened recently. Fields Medalist Peter Scholze came up with a revolutionary new framework called *Condensed Mathematics*, which is a direct modern evolution of Grothendieck’s ideas on geometry and topology.
The proof of his foundational theorem was so complex and abstract that Scholze himself admitted he wanted a machine to verify it to be absolutely certain it was correct.
 * **The Challenge:** Translate Grothendieck-style homological algebra into code.
 * **The Result:** A team of mathematicians, led by Kevin Buzzard and Johan Commelin, used the **Lean** proof assistant to completely formalize and verify the proof.
The computer proved that these dizzyingly abstract, "Grothendieckian" concepts could be broken down into the simple, binary, logical steps of a Turing machine.
### 3. Automating Symbolic Transformations
You mentioned the automation of symbolic transformations, and that is where the future gets really interesting. Grothendieck's work often requires keeping track of massive, multi-dimensional diagrams of arrows (morphisms) that must commute (equal each other no matter which path you take).
For a human, drawing and verifying these diagrams is exhausting and prone to minor clerical errors. For a computer, it’s a graph theory problem. Programs are now being developed to automatically rewrite and simplify these categorical diagrams, effectively doing the heavy lifting of Grothendieck's structural transformations automatically.
> **The Great Irony:** Grothendieck famously despised computers and feared that technology would alienate humanity from nature. Yet, his mathematics is so purely logical and structural that it might ultimately find its most permanent, flawless home inside the memory banks of silicon machines.
